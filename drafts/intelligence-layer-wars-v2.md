# The Intelligence Layer Wars

*Draft v2 for The Full Stack — July 2026*

---

I was on a call last week with a CMO—I'll call her Sarah, because she asked me not to use her name—who'd just finished a bake-off between four marketing automation platforms. Salesforce, Adobe, HubSpot, and one of the AI-native challengers. She'd seen every demo. Sat through every roadmap presentation. Watched each vendor's agent assemble a campaign in what they all promised would be "minutes, not days."

"They all did the same thing," she told me. "Same models underneath—I asked. OpenAI or Anthropic. The interfaces were different. The branding was different. But when I pushed on what made them actually different, nobody had a good answer."

She paused.

"So I'm supposed to pick based on... what, exactly? Who has the nicer dashboard?"

---

This is what market commoditization looks like when it happens in real time. And Sarah's question—what are we actually choosing between?—is the one nobody in the vendor pitch meetings wants to answer honestly.

DMNews ran a piece last week that put numbers to what Sarah was experiencing. Ninety percent of marketing organizations now use AI agents somewhere in their stack. Not experimenting. Using. Every vendor has converged on the same pitch: describe your campaign, the agent builds it, you launch in minutes. Gartner says 40% of enterprise applications will embed agents by year-end, up from under 5% last year.

That's fast. It's also—as the DMNews piece put it—already table stakes. "Our AI launches campaigns fast" stopped being a differentiator around Q1. It's the new free shipping.

---

Here's where it gets interesting.

I've been working on this framework with Martin Kihn—we're calling it the Agentic Management Platform, which is a mouthful, but it captures something important. The architecture stacks in three layers: Identity and Governance at the bottom. Intelligence in the middle. Agent Orchestration at the top.

The orchestration layer is where the agents live. It's where the demos happen, where the "minutes not days" magic gets performed. It's also, increasingly, where the commodity action is. Same models. Same speeds. Same capabilities wrapped in different UIs.

The intelligence layer is different. That's the unified data fabric—the business semantics, the knowledge graph, the memory of what actually worked. It's where the DMP and the CDP get absorbed into something bigger. Not disappearing, but becoming components of a richer infrastructure.

When I first sketched this out, I thought the orchestration layer would be the battleground. The place where vendors competed. That's what the press releases say. That's what the demo theater suggests.

But Sarah's bake-off tells a different story. The orchestration layer is converging. The intelligence layer is diverging. The enterprises building proprietary context—first-party data they actually own, decision logic that's actually theirs—are the ones creating durable advantage. Everyone else is renting the same reasoning engine and hoping the logo on the interface makes a difference.

The market is validating the architecture faster than I expected. Just sayin'.

---

There's a second shift that the vendor speed race tends to obscure, and it's the one that keeps me up at night.

ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Gemini—these are increasingly where buyers research and evaluate brands before any marketing campaign reaches them. McKinsey estimates 20-50% of traffic from traditional search is at risk. The buyer's agent doesn't see your campaign. It doesn't care about your creative. It evaluates your data, your structured knowledge, your trustworthiness as a source.

I asked Sarah about this. Had she thought about how her own customers' agents would find and evaluate her company?

Long pause.

"That's... not what we were baking off."

Right. She was optimizing the part of the stack that's commoditizing. The part that determines whether her brand shows up in the first place—the intelligence layer—wasn't even in the conversation.

---

When we pitched *Agent Driven* to Wiley, the question that kept coming up was timing. Are we ahead of the market? Is this architecture theoretical, or is it actually happening?

I think Sarah answered that question. The enterprises that built robust intelligence layers are winning agent evaluations. The ones wrapping commodity AI in branded interfaces are discovering what she discovered: speed without context doesn't compound.

The frameworks in the book—the AMP architecture, the TIE Fighter evolution, the Three Horizons investment model—they're not predictions anymore. They're descriptions of what's happening right now.

That's the pitch. That's the timing. And that's why the book matters more this quarter than it did last quarter.
